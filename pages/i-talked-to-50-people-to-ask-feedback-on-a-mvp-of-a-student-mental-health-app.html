50 Conversations That Changed My Confidence

March 12, 2026 · 4 min read

If you want to build a startup, you cannot avoid talking to people. You have to explain ideas, ask questions, and get feedback from strangers. But many of us freeze when conversations start.

I faced the same problem. So I decided to challenge myself I talked to 50 strangers around my campus and asked for feedback on a small MVP. The goal wasn't perfection. It was discomfort, practice, and eventually, growth.

In the video below, I share what I learned from that experiment and a simple way anyone can practice starting conversations without feeling awkward.

Conversation Starters That Actually Work

The hardest part isn't the conversation it's the first sentence. Here are the openers I tested, in both English and Hinglish, that worked the best on campus.

English Openers

  • →Hey, a quick question for you.
  • →Hi, can I have your opinion on this app?
  • →Hey, are you a student here?
  • →Hey bro, I'm testing a small idea… can I get one minute?
  • →Excuse me, can I ask something quickly?
  • →Hi, do you have one minute?
  • →Hey, can I show you something quickly?

Hinglish Openers

  • →Ek minute mil sakta hai apka? Ek chhota sa question tha.
  • →Ek cheez par aapka opinion chahiye tha… Can I get one minute?
  • →Aap yahin ke student ho kya?
  • →Bro ek chhota idea test kar raha hoon… Can I get your opinion?
  • →Excuse me, jaldi se ek cheez pooch sakta hoon?
  • →Main students se ek interesting cheez pooch raha hoon…

How to End a Conversation Gracefully

Closings matter just as much as openers. A good close leaves the person feeling respected and leaves you feeling confident. Here's what I used:

English Closers

  • →Thanks for your time.
  • →It actually helped me a lot.
  • →Thanks for the suggestions… I will consider them.
  • →I'll work on what you said. Thank you.

Hinglish Closers

  • →Batane ke liye thanks.
  • →Isko improve karne ki try karunga.
  • →Time and suggestion dene ke liye thank you.

You walked in as a stranger. You walked out with a lesson. That's the whole game.
