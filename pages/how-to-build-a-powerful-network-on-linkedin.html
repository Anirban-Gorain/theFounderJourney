Secret 1: Send Your Animals to the Right Forest

The main idea is to treat your skills like animals and send them to the right forest to hunt. On LinkedIn, this means connecting only with people and groups where your skills can add some value.

Step 1: Understand Connection Levels

LinkedIn has three levels of connections:

  • 1st-degree connections: People you are already connected with. You can message them directly.
    Example: Your batchmates, colleagues, or people you’ve already connected with.
  • 2nd-degree connections: People connected to your 1st-degree connections but not to you but you can send them a connection request.
  • 3rd-degree connections: People connected to your 2nd-degree connections they are more far away from you. Their profile visibility can be limited to you depending on their privacy settings.

Why this matters: Start building your forest gradually, start sending connection requests 2nd and 3rd-degree connections it will expand you connection.

Step 2: Sending Connection Requests

LinkedIn limits the number of connection requests you can send per week to prevent spam. You can send around 100 requests per week, which means 400 requests per month. Honestly speaking, this is more than enough. Let me give you a small example:

If you target 4 companies each month, 400 connections per month ÷ 4 = 100 requests for each company. Even though 30% of people may not accept your request, you will still have 60–70 connections in each company/community. In Secret-2 you can use these connections strategically.

Tips: Always add a personalized-note. Don’t just click “Connect”.

Example personalized note:
Hi [Name], I saw your post about [topic] and found it really insightful. I’d love to connect and learn more about your experience in [field].

Why personalized messages work: Increases acceptance rate and shows you are serious.

Step 3: Find Your Forest – People and Groups

A. People (individuals)

Use LinkedIn search: type your skill/job keyword → People tab → Filters (2nd/3rd-degree, company, location).

Connecting with irrelevant people reduces your chances of getting help or referrals.

B. Groups (communities)

Use the LinkedIn search bar to add relevant keywords, then filter by Groups. Join the groups that match your interests or industry.

    How to pick groups: Check name & description, activity level, avoid promotional or marketing groups.

    Secret 2 – Play the Number Game

    Maximize your chances of getting replies or referrals by messaging a large number of relevant people in your existing network, specifically in the same company where the job is posted.

    Step 1: Identify the Opportunity

    Find a job that interests you anywhere. Example: Content Creator role at Microsoft. Apply first on LinkedIn or the company website.

    Concept: A single application rarely gets attention; messaging multiple people in your network improves your probability of a response.

    Step 2: Identify Relevant People in Your Network

    Use LinkedIn search: type company name. Example: Microsoft . Filter → People → 1st-degree connections. Start sending the you referral message template to as many connections as you can. Make sure you fix sir/mam based on gender, Morning or evening based on when you are sending the message.

    Step 6: Track Responses

    Maintain a simple tracker (Notion, Google Sheet, or notebook) with columns: Name | Company | Role | Date Messaged | Response. Follow up 1–2 weeks later if no reply.

    Summary – Messaging Game

    • Apply for the job first.
    • Identify all 1st-degree connections in the same company, ideally in the same field but include other departments too.
    • Send personalized, concise messages highlighting your value.
    • Track responses and follow up → maximize chances of success.

    Key idea: Here the focus is not on connecting (in Secret you already did it), but leveraging your closest network to broadcast your application broadly and increase reply probability.

    LinkedIn Networking Tips

    Do not say “Thank You”

    Instead, say: “I appreciate that you told me”

    Why: It adds a personal touch and shows you genuinely value the interaction, not just following formalities.

    Do not chase people who are too far ahead

    Avoid reaching out to ultra-experienced professionals with decades of experience, they are less likely to reply and may not relate to your level.

    Focus on people 2–3 years ahead of you. They are approachable, understand your challenges, and can mentor or refer you effectively.

    Example: If you’re a junior content creator, connect with someone who has been in the role for 2–3 years rather than a VP-level executive.

    Some people are there to market, not mentor

    Many professionals post content to promote courses, webinars, or products. Their primary goal is visibility, not mentorship.

    Avoid expecting guidance or favors from them they rarely provide meaningful 1:1 help.

    Focus on people who actively share experiences, give advice but not a influencer, or respond thoughtfully in comments.

    Talk where everyone can see you

    Don’t just scroll. Visibility builds credibility. Engage in comments, share insights, ask thoughtful questions.

    Do not keep changing your DP

    A consistent profile picture helps people recognize you over time, which is essential for networking.

    Tip: Choose a professional, friendly, and clear image that represents you well and stick with it.

    Connections matter more than likes

    Focus on building a network of helpful connections—people who can provide advice, referrals, or guidance.

    Tip: Even if your post gets few likes, having the right 50 connections actively looking out for you is far more valuable than 500 passive followers.

    Do not wait to be noticed

    Instead of waiting for people to approach you, take initiative. Ask for openings, mentorship, collaboration this will set you apart from thr crowd.
